# Transcript: Overcoming Team Friction & Bridging Personalities
**Speaker:** Cameron Hammond  
**Topic:** Team Collaboration and Soft Skills  
**Context:** Junior Core Program, BYU Information Systems

---

## The Challenge: A Personality Mismatch
During my junior year in the **Information Systems Junior Core at BYU**, our cohort was placed into teams based on personality surveys and algorithms designed to group similar individuals. Humorously, our group quickly realized we were nothing alike. While other teams were marveling at their similarities, we were a group of outliers wondering what we had in common—other than the drive to get the work done.

### The Conflict: "The Bubbly Guy" vs. "The Stoic"
One of my teammates—whom we’ll call **John**—was a very stoic, hardworking individual. He was a former linebacker, physically imposing, and incredibly disciplined in both his academics and fitness. 

My personality is naturally outgoing; I’m "bubbly," I like to talk, and I believe that getting to know people on a personal level is the best way to build trust in a team. However, my typical tactics for building rapport didn't work with John. He didn't care for small talk or sharing stories about his weekend; he wanted to produce high-quality work quickly and efficiently.

---

## The Turning Point: Adjusting My Approach
I realized that my way of building trust wasn't "inherently right" for everyone. I had to undergo a learning curve and recognize that John wouldn't respond to my usual social outreach. 

### Key Shifts in Strategy:
* **Respecting Boundaries:** I stopped trying to force "chit-chat" and started respecting his preference for focused, quiet work.
* **Building Trust through Competence:** I learned that for some people, trust isn't built through shared stories, but through **shared excellence**. We began to understand each other by simply doing good work together.
* **Recognizing Value:** I grew to appreciate his stoicism and his drive for quality, rather than seeing his lack of talkativeness as a barrier.

---

## Results and Success
By the end of the semester, we faced a massive, week-long capstone project. At the start of the year, the idea of tackling such a project with someone so different from me was daunting. However, because we had developed a mutual understanding of how to work together, we performed exceptionally well.

* **Award-Winning Output:** Our team was awarded **Second Place** for our project, which included a custom-built website, technical analysis, and a formal presentation.
* **Long-Term Collaboration:** The partnership was so successful that we actually chose to team up again the following semester and the semester after that. 

---

## "The So What" / Lessons Learned
This experience taught me that "difficult" teammates are often just "different" teammates. Success in a professional environment isn't about finding people exactly like you; it’s about having the emotional intelligence to adapt your communication style to meet the needs of the person across the table. 

Years later, I see this as a foundational skill for my career. Whether working with engineers or clients, I know how to identify what someone values—whether that's personal connection or technical efficiency—and pivot my approach to ensure we deliver high-quality results.
