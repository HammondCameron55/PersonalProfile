# Transcript: AIS National Competition (Logan, UT)
**Speaker:** Cameron Hammond  
**Date of Event:** Winter/Spring 2024  
**Source:** Personal Reflection for RAG Database

## Introduction
Hello, my name is **Cameron Hammond**, and this recording is to create a transcript for the RAG searchable database for my personal website. This is the story of attending the **National Association for Information Systems (AIS)** event in the winter and spring of 2024. It was a fantastic event during my junior year in the Junior Core. 

My teammates—**Nia Croft, Adam Kelly, and Ethan Nance**—and I heard about the event and signed up. We were ultimately selected as one of the teams to represent **BYU**. 

---

## The Project: Socioeconomic Interconnectedness
The project involved receiving a dataset to analyze and developing actionable items based on that data. The core goal was to find ways to increase **social interconnectedness** between different socioeconomic groups. To put it simply: the goal was to help kids from lower-income backgrounds find friends in higher-income brackets, and vice versa, because these groups are typically divided. We wanted to help create that interconnectedness.

### Analysis and Methodology
We began by analyzing the data using various tools we learned in our **Machine Learning** class. We worked on our analysis until we gave a preliminary presentation over Zoom. We were eventually selected to go to the finals, which was a huge honor.

---

## The Finals in Logan, Utah
The finals were held at **Utah State University** in Logan, Utah. It was a bit funny because Logan is only about a two-and-a-half-hour drive from where we live and go to school, and usually, BYU trips involve flying to different states. Regardless, it was a great opportunity.

### Preparation and Mentorship
We got the presentation down pat. It was a difficult task, but we became skilled at presenting thanks to the help of our professors. I specifically worked with **Dr. Mark Keith** and **Professor Katie Reese**. Professor Reese was probably the biggest help; she advised us and completely transformed our slide deck and our overall presentation style.

---

## The Competition Experience
When we arrived in Logan, the event provided dinner, and then the day of the competition came. We had been practicing late into the night. By the morning of the event, we knew our parts—and each other’s parts—extremely well. 

We were nervous, but confident in our preparation and our recommendations. We truly believed our analysis would lead to real outcomes. When we met the judges and presented, it was one of those unique cases where we performed exactly as we had practiced. Usually, after a performance, you think about what you could have done better, but this time we felt we did the very best we could with the time we had.

---

## Feedback from Industry Leaders
The judges were impressed. One judge, **Brandon Wood from Snowflake**, gave us glowing praise afterward. He told us:

> "Of all the presentations we saw today, yours was the one most focused on actual outcomes. Rather than spending time on the technical details of the analysis or the technologies used, you focused on the 'so what' of the situation."

He noted that in the real world, that is what people care about: what the analysis means for them and what can be done about it. That praise meant a lot to me and the entire team.

---

## Networking and Global Connections
We also met amazing people. **Professor Spencer Hilton** pointed out something unique: while all the students were eating together, the BYU students were uniquely capable of being outgoing and courteous. People were drawn to us and joined our conversations. 

As we strove to be gregarious, people responded well. We made friends with people from all across the country and the world. Even though it was a national competition, many students were from international backgrounds, originally from Africa and Asia. 

Other students even asked us for honest feedback on their own presentations. They didn't necessarily think we were more skilled, but they trusted that we would answer them honestly and kindly. That was a huge blessing. During the trip, our team also visited the **Logan Temple** to do baptisms, which was a great experience.

---

## Conclusion and Career Impact
To put it in "resume terms": my team joined a competition to analyze, clean, and extract actionable items from a dataset. Our analysis was sound, our recommendations were practical for a school board to implement, and our presentation was excellent.

We did end up receiving an award. I’m grateful for the professors and my team. This experience boosted my confidence in performing difficult analyses and high-stakes presentations. 

Now, as I prepare to be a **Sales Engineer**, I realize the principles are the same. You have to speak to what the customer really wants to know—the "so what." They don't care about the nitty-gritty details of the technology; they want to know the impact it has and how it helps people.

To end, it is important to point out that we won 2nd place at the competition! Yay!
